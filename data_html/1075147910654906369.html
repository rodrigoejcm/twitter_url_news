Health chiefs in desperate plea for organ donations from ethnic minorities

EXCLUSIVE A chronic shortage of organs for ethnic minorities is causing a higher death rate

  • Share
  •  
  •  Comments
By
Martin BagotHealth and Science Correspondent
  • 21:55, 18 DEC 2018

An appeal has been launched for more organ donors from ethnic minorities.

NHS figures show these communities make up 11% of the population yet represent 35% of those on the kidney waiting list.

Asian patients wait an average of three years for a new lung, compared to eight months for those who are white.

Anthony Clarkson, of NHS Blood and Transplant, said: Although many black, Asian and ethnic minority patients are able to receive a transplant from a white donor, others may die if there is no donor from their own community.

He wants more from these communities to talk about donation and [to] share their decision with their families.

About 41% from those backgrounds consent to be donors, compared with 69% of the white population. The Mirror is campaigning for new rules in England.

The proposed law, currently going through Parliament, would result in people being presumed as consenting to be organ donors unless they opt out.

'This can be prevented'

Mazim Ali, who had a kidney transplant in 2013, raises awareness in the Asian community of the need for organ donors.

The 51-year-old dad, of Birmingham, said: [People have] misconceptions... about whether their religion allows organ donation... This is a preventable situation.
